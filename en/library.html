Fragments of an Unfulfilled Fate

Sometimes life does not break.
It simply fails to come together — in parts, in decisions, in moments.

Without drama.
Without a final point.

There are places where you could have turned.
There are seconds when the choice is already made,
even though you have not realized it yet.

This book is neither a story nor an explanation.
It is three worlds where everything begins lightly:
depth, heat, love.
And in each of them, you do not pay immediately.

There are no heroes and no villains here.
There are scenes that return later —
already without scenery.

Open the fragment.
What follows is the moment after which
you want to read on,
just to understand what stayed with you.

At some point you begin to understand:
it is not that the story ended.
It is that it never came together.

Everything felt like life.
Depth, where you made choices without thinking they could be final.
Heat, where illusions looked like freedom.
Love — so beautiful that you wanted to believe in it more than in yourself.

There were no catastrophes in these stories.
No villains.
Almost no mistakes.

There were moments when you went a little further than you should have.
Or stopped — too early.

The strangest thing is that the women in these stories do not disappear.
Something else disappears:
the context in which you were needed,
the role you were expected to play,
the feeling that all of this was leading somewhere.

And when the scenery is gone,
silence remains.
Not empty.
Just honest.

It is from such pauses
that the fragments of an unfulfilled fate are formed.

Asia. Women

Asia always begins the same way.
With heat, smells, movement —
and the feeling that everything can be different here.

No unnecessary questions are asked here.
It is easy to be who you once wanted to appear as:
free, interesting, needed.

The first conversations are simple.
The first women are warm.
The first decisions are quick.

And at some point you catch yourself thinking
that you are back on stage.
You are expected. You are welcome.
Your silence is taken for depth,
your gestures for generosity,
your departure for mystery.

This book is not about exoticism
and not about love.

It is about the moment
when Asia stops being a background
and becomes a mirror.
When you suddenly realize:
you are not paying with money —
you are paying with yourself.

There are no answers here.
But there are points
after which the illusion of “starting over”
no longer works the same way.

The fragment is not a promise.
It is a test.

Everything in Asia happens faster.
Not because people rush — on the contrary,
no one is in a hurry.

You simply become lighter.
The past holds you less,
the future binds you less.

Here you can disappear for several days —
and it will not seem strange.
Here you can return —
and be accepted
as if you never left.

I remember one evening.
We were sitting on plastic chairs by the road,
drinking something sweet with ice,
and she calmly spoke about her family —
without complaints, without strain.

I listened and caught myself on a strange feeling:
I felt good not because I was next to a woman,
but because I was back in context.

I was needed again.
Not as a person —
but as movement.
As a possibility.

And then, for the first time, a question appears
that is never asked out loud:

what will remain
if this city,
this evening,
and this woman disappear at the same time?
